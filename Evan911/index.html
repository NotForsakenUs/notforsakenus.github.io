Evangelism - Wednesday, September 11, 2024

What do you think evangelism is?

Evangelism is that Spirit-empowered activity whereby all disciples of Jesus Christ give an intentional, complete, and verbal witness to his life, death, burial, and resurrection, exhorting unbelievers to repent of their sins and place their faith in Christ alone for salvation so that they may become his baptized, obedient disciples.

Evangelism is Spirit-empowered.

Do not underestimate the power of the Holy Spirit to move.

1 - Impulsive and rash: Peter often acted or spoke without thinking things through. For example, he impulsively cut off the ear of the high priest's servant during Jesus' arrest (John 18:10-11).

2 - Overconfident: Peter boasted that he would never deny Jesus, even if all others did. However, he ended up denying Jesus three times, just as Jesus had predicted (Matthew 26:33-35, 69-75).

3 - Lacks faith at times: Despite walking on water toward Jesus, Peter became afraid and began to sink when he saw the wind and waves (Matthew 14:28-31).

4 - Fears man at times: Peter denied knowing Jesus three times out of fear for his own safety during Jesus' trial (Luke 22:54-62).

5 - Inconsistent: Peter sometimes wavered in his convictions. For instance, after receiving a vision from God about accepting Gentiles, he later withdrew from eating with them when certain Jewish believers arrived (Galatians 2:11-14).

6 - Speaks before thinking: On multiple occasions, Peter spoke out of turn or said inappropriate things. One example is when he rebuked Jesus for predicting His own death, prompting Jesus to say, "Get behind me, Satan!" (Mark 8:32-33).

If we approach evangelism with a total reliance on the Holy Spirit and not ourselves, it lessens the burden and anxiety we feel.

All Disciples of Jesus Christ are Called to Evangelism

“Jesus expects everyone who identifies as his disciple to make disciples. He never delimited the obligation of disciple-making only to a particular segment or group--every disciple bears this responsibility” - p. 36.

Why do you think so many of us do not evangelize regularly?

“Reaching all people requires the evangelistic participation of all disciples.

The world cannot and will not be won to faith in Christ by only those who serve as ministers and missionaries. The number of un-believers in the world exponentially outnumbers the size of the Christian ministerial ranks.

To reach as many as possible with the gospel, every disciple—new and mature, young and old, male and female, evangelistically experienced and inexperienced—must be employed into Christ’s disciple-making enterprise” - p. 37.

The third point the author makes is that evangelism should “give an intentional, complete, and verbal witness.”

“The exercise of evangelism requires its practitioners to be intentional and deliberate. As mentioned in the previous chapter, while some opportunities to evangelize may come at times when believers do not expect them, evangelism will never, ever occur by accident.

Believers must predetermine they will be obedient to share the gospel during specific times they have scheduled in their calendar, as well as those unscheduled times when the Spirit spontaneously prompts them to evangelize another person” p. 37.

“Focusing on the need to communicate the evangelistic message does not negate the necessity for believers’ lives and actions to match their evangelistic speech. Moral living—on its own merits and apart from verbalizing the gospel—provides only as much, if not less, an explanation of the gospel than creation generally reveals the existence of a creator God” p. 38.

“I heard the story of a man who became a Christian during an evangelistic emphasis in a city in the Pacific Northwest.

When the man told his boss about it, his employer responded with: “That’s great! I am a Christian and have been praying for you for years!”

But the new believer was crestfallen. “Why didn’t you ever tell me you were a Christian? You were the very reason I have not been interested in the gospel all these years.”

“How can that be?” the boss wondered. “I have done my very best to live the Christian life around you.”

“That’s the point,” explained the employee. “You lived such a model life without telling me it was Christ who made the difference, I convinced myself that if you could live such a good and happy life without Christ, then I could, too.”

Here are a few practical ways we can be intentional in evangelism:

1 - Pray regularly for opportunities to share your faith and for God to open doors for gospel conversations.

2 - Be prepared to give an answer about your faith by studying apologetics and practicing sharing your testimony (your 3 min. story).

3 - Intentionally build relationships with non-believers and look for natural ways to bring up spritual topics (10,000 transforming relationships).

4 - Set specific goals for evangelism, such as aiming to share the gospel once a day or once a week.

5 - Invite non-Christian friends and acquaintances to church events or your life group.

6 - Learn to recognize and act on the prompting of the Holy Spirit to share your faith in everyday situations.

7 - Partner with other believers for accountability and encouragement in evangelism.

8 - Look for ways to serve others and meet needs in the community as a bridge to sharing the gospel.

9 - Carry gospel tracts or other evangelistic materials to give out when opportunities arise.

10 - Be intentional about mentioning your faith and church involvement in conversations.
